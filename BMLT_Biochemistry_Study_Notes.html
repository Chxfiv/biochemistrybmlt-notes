1.1 INTRODUCTION TO CARBOHYDRATES

Definition: Carbohydrates are polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or compounds that yield such substances on hydrolysis. They are the most abundant organic compounds in nature.
General Formula: (CH‚ÇÇO)‚Çô where n ‚â• 3 Alternative Formula: C‚Çô(H‚ÇÇO)‚Çô

Etymology and Historical Background

The term "carbohydrate" was coined by Carl Schmidt in 1844, literally meaning "carbon with water" (carbon + hydrate). Early chemists observed that many sugars had the empirical formula (CH‚ÇÇO)‚Çô, suggesting they were hydrates of carbon.

Classification of Carbohydrates

Based on Number of Sugar Units:

  • Monosaccharides: Single sugar units (glucose, fructose, galactose)
  • Disaccharides: Two sugar units (sucrose, lactose, maltose)
  • Oligosaccharides: 3-10 sugar units (raffinose, stachyose)
  • Polysaccharides: Many sugar units (starch, glycogen, cellulose)

Based on Functional Groups:

  • Aldoses: Contain aldehyde group (-CHO)
  • Ketoses: Contain ketone group (C=O)

Based on Number of Carbon Atoms:

  • Trioses: 3 carbons (glyceraldehyde, dihydroxyacetone)
  • Tetroses: 4 carbons (erythrose, threose)
  • Pentoses: 5 carbons (ribose, xylose, arabinose)
  • Hexoses: 6 carbons (glucose, fructose, galactose)
  • Heptoses: 7 carbons (sedoheptulose)
Clinical Significance: Carbohydrates serve as the primary energy source for cellular metabolism, providing 4 kcal/g. They are essential for brain function, as the brain preferentially uses glucose for energy.

1.2 MONOSACCHARIDES

Monosaccharides are the simplest carbohydrates that cannot be hydrolyzed into smaller carbohydrate units. They are the building blocks of all other carbohydrates.

Key Properties:

  • Optical Activity: All monosaccharides except dihydroxyacetone are optically active
  • Reducing Nature: Free aldehyde or ketone groups can reduce Benedict's and Fehling's reagents
  • Mutarotation: Spontaneous change in optical rotation due to Œ± and Œ≤ anomer interconversion
  • Solubility: Highly soluble in water due to multiple hydroxyl groups

Important Monosaccharides

1. D-Glucose (Dextrose)

  • Most abundant monosaccharide in nature
  • Normal blood glucose: 70-100 mg/dL (3.9-5.6 mmol/L)
  • Primary fuel for brain and red blood cells
  • Stored as glycogen in liver and muscles

2. D-Fructose (Levulose)

  • Sweetest naturally occurring sugar
  • Found in fruits and honey
  • Metabolized primarily in liver
  • Does not require insulin for cellular uptake

3. D-Galactose

  • Component of lactose (milk sugar)
  • Converted to glucose in liver via Leloir pathway
  • Deficiency in galactokinase causes galactosemia

4. D-Ribose

  • Component of RNA, ATP, NAD, FAD
  • Essential for nucleic acid synthesis
  • Produced via pentose phosphate pathway
Laboratory Tests:
  • Benedict's Test: Detects reducing sugars (positive for glucose, fructose, galactose)
  • Glucose Oxidase Method: Specific for glucose detection
  • Seliwanoff's Test: Specific for ketoses (fructose gives cherry red color)

1.3 STEREOCHEMISTRY OF CARBOHYDRATES

Stereochemistry deals with the spatial arrangement of atoms in molecules. In carbohydrates, stereochemistry is crucial because biological systems are highly stereospecific.

Chirality and Optical Activity

  • Chiral Carbon: Carbon atom bonded to four different groups
  • Enantiomers: Non-superimposable mirror images
  • Diastereomers: Stereoisomers that are not mirror images
  • Epimers: Diastereomers differing at only one chiral center

  • Trehalase Deficiency: Mushroom intolerance

1.5 POLYSACCHARIDES

Polysaccharides are complex carbohydrates composed of many monosaccharide units (>10) linked by glycosidic bonds. They serve structural and energy storage functions.

Storage Polysaccharides

1. Starch (Plant Storage)

  • Amylose (20-25%):
    • Linear polymer of glucose with Œ±(1‚Üí4) bonds
    • Molecular weight: 10,000-100,000 Da
    • Helical structure, blue color with iodine
  • Amylopectin (75-80%):
    • Branched polymer with Œ±(1‚Üí4) backbone and Œ±(1‚Üí6) branches
    • Branch points every 25-30 glucose units
    • Molecular weight: 300,000-3,000,000 Da
    • Purple color with iodine

2. Glycogen (Animal Storage)

  • Highly branched glucose polymer
  • Œ±(1‚Üí4) backbone with Œ±(1‚Üí6) branches every 8-12 units
  • Stored in liver (100-120g) and skeletal muscle (300-600g)
  • Rapid mobilization for energy during fasting
  • Molecular weight: 1,000,000-10,000,000 Da

Structural Polysaccharides

1. Cellulose

  • Linear polymer of glucose with Œ≤(1‚Üí4) bonds
  • Most abundant organic compound on Earth
  • Indigestible by humans (lack cellulase enzyme)
  • Important dietary fiber

2. Chitin

  • Polymer of N-acetylglucosamine with Œ≤(1‚Üí4) bonds
  • Found in arthropod exoskeletons and fungal cell walls
  • Second most abundant polysaccharide

3. Hyaluronic Acid

  • Glycosaminoglycan in connective tissue
  • Alternating glucuronic acid and N-acetylglucosamine
  • High water-binding capacity
Glycogen Storage Diseases:
  • Type I (von Gierke): Glucose-6-phosphatase deficiency
  • Type II (Pompe): Œ±-1,4-Glucosidase deficiency
  • Type III (Cori): Debranching enzyme deficiency
  • Type IV (Andersen): Branching enzyme deficiency

MODULE 2: PROTEINS

2.1 INTRODUCTION TO PROTEINS

Proteins are complex macromolecules composed of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. They are the most diverse and functionally important biological molecules, performing virtually all cellular functions.

Historical Background

The term "protein" was coined by J√∂ns Jacob Berzelius in 1838, derived from the Greek word "proteios" meaning "primary" or "of first importance." Proteins were first recognized as a distinct class of biological molecules in the 18th century.

Functions of Proteins

1. Catalytic Function (Enzymes)

  • Accelerate biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy
  • Examples: Pepsin (digestion), Catalase (H‚ÇÇO‚ÇÇ breakdown)
  • Highly specific for substrates and reaction conditions

2. Structural Function

  • Provide mechanical support and shape to cells and tissues
  • Examples: Collagen (connective tissue), Keratin (hair, nails)
  • Elastin (elastic fibers), Actin and Myosin (muscle)

3. Transport Function

  • Carry molecules across membranes or through body fluids
  • Examples: Hemoglobin (oxygen), Albumin (fatty acids)
  • Transferrin (iron), Lipoproteins (lipids)

4. Defense Function

  • Protect against pathogens and foreign substances
  • Examples: Immunoglobulins (antibodies), Complement proteins
  • Lysozyme (antimicrobial), Fibrinogen (blood clotting)

5. Regulatory Function

  • Control metabolic processes and gene expression
  • Examples: Insulin (glucose metabolism), Growth hormone
  • Transcription factors, Regulatory enzymes

6. Storage Function

  • Store amino acids and other molecules
  • Examples: Casein (milk protein), Ovalbumin (egg white)
  • Ferritin (iron storage), Myoglobin (oxygen storage)
Clinical Significance: Protein analysis is fundamental in clinical diagnosis. Total protein, albumin, and specific enzymes serve as biomarkers for various diseases including liver dysfunction, kidney disease, and tissue damage.

2.3 PEPTIDE BONDS AND PROTEIN STRUCTURE

Peptide Bond is a covalent bond formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another amino acid through dehydration synthesis.

Peptide Bond Formation

Peptide Bond Formation: Amino Acid 1 + Amino Acid 2 ‚Üí Dipeptide + H‚ÇÇO NH‚ÇÇ NH‚ÇÇ NH‚ÇÇ | | | H‚ÇÇN-C-COOH + H‚ÇÇN-C-COOH ‚Üí H‚ÇÇN-C-CO-NH-C-COOH + H‚ÇÇO | | | | R‚ÇÅ R‚ÇÇ R‚ÇÅ R‚ÇÇ ‚Üë PEPTIDE BOND

Characteristics of Peptide Bond:

  • Partial double bond character: C-N bond length = 1.32 √Ö
  • Planar structure: No free rotation around C-N bond
  • Trans configuration: More stable than cis (except proline)
  • Resonance stabilization: Contributes to protein stability
  • Polar nature: Can form hydrogen bonds

Levels of Protein Structure

1. Primary Structure (1¬∞)

  • Linear sequence of amino acids in polypeptide chain
  • Determined by genetic code (DNA ‚Üí RNA ‚Üí Protein)
  • Held together by peptide bonds
  • Determines all higher levels of structure
  • Example: Insulin A-chain has 21 amino acids

2. Secondary Structure (2¬∞)

  • Local folding patterns stabilized by hydrogen bonds
  • Œ±-Helix:
    • Right-handed spiral structure
    • 3.6 amino acids per turn
    • Hydrogen bonds between C=O and N-H groups
    • Common in fibrous proteins
  • Œ≤-Sheet:
    • Extended polypeptide chains
    • Parallel or antiparallel arrangement
    • Hydrogen bonds between adjacent strands
    • Pleated sheet appearance
  • Œ≤-Turn: Reversal of polypeptide direction
  • Random coil: Irregular, flexible regions

3. Tertiary Structure (3¬∞)

  • Overall three-dimensional shape of single polypeptide
  • Stabilized by various interactions:
    • Hydrogen bonds: Between polar side chains
    • Ionic bonds: Between charged residues (salt bridges)
    • Van der Waals forces: Weak attractions
    • Hydrophobic interactions: Nonpolar residues cluster
    • Disulfide bonds: Covalent bonds between cysteine residues
  • Determines biological activity

4. Quaternary Structure (4¬∞)

  • Association of multiple polypeptide subunits
  • Stabilized by non-covalent interactions
  • Examples:
    • Hemoglobin: 2Œ± + 2Œ≤ subunits
    • Immunoglobulin G: 2 heavy + 2 light chains
    • Lactate dehydrogenase: 4 subunits
Clinical Example - Sickle Cell Anemia:
  • Primary: Glu6Val mutation in Œ≤-globin chain
  • Secondary: Altered local folding around mutation
  • Tertiary: Hydrophobic patch formation on protein surface
  • Quaternary: Abnormal hemoglobin polymerization
  • Result: Red cell sickling and vaso-occlusive crises

2.4 PROTEIN DENATURATION

Protein Denaturation is the disruption of protein structure (secondary, tertiary, quaternary) without breaking peptide bonds, resulting in loss of biological activity.

Denaturing Agents

1. Physical Agents

  • Heat: Increases molecular motion, breaks weak bonds
  • Radiation: UV, X-rays cause structural damage
  • Mechanical stress: Shearing forces
  • Pressure: High pressure changes protein conformation

2. Chemical Agents

  • pH extremes: Alter ionization states, disrupt salt bridges
  • Organic solvents: Ethanol, acetone disrupt hydrophobic interactions
  • Detergents: SDS disrupts protein structure
  • Chaotropic agents: Urea, guanidine HCl break hydrogen bonds
  • Heavy metals: Hg¬≤‚Å∫, Pb¬≤‚Å∫ bind to sulfur atoms
  • Reducing agents: Œ≤-mercaptoethanol breaks disulfide bonds

Types of Denaturation

Reversible Denaturation (Renaturation)

  • Mild denaturing conditions
  • Protein can refold to native state
  • Example: Ribonuclease refolding
  • Requires proper conditions (pH, temperature, ionic strength)

Irreversible Denaturation

  • Harsh denaturing conditions
  • Permanent loss of native structure
  • Example: Egg white coagulation by heat
  • Often involves aggregation or precipitation

Laboratory Applications

Protein Precipitation Methods

  • Trichloroacetic acid (TCA): Protein purification
  • Ammonium sulfate: Salting out proteins
  • Acetone: Organic solvent precipitation
  • Heat: Coagulation studies

Electrophoresis Applications

  • SDS-PAGE: SDS denatures proteins for size separation
  • Urea-PAGE: Urea for native gel electrophoresis
  • Isoelectric focusing: pH gradient denaturation
Clinical Significance:
  • Fever effects: High temperature can denature enzymes
  • Protein aggregation diseases: Alzheimer's, Parkinson's
  • Laboratory tests: Heat coagulation test for proteins

2.5 ENZYMES - BIOLOGICAL CATALYSTS

Enzymes are biological catalysts that accelerate biochemical reactions by lowering the activation energy. They are predominantly proteins with high specificity for their substrates.

Enzyme Classification (EC System)

1. Oxidoreductases (EC 1)

  • Catalyze oxidation-reduction reactions
  • Examples: Lactate dehydrogenase, Catalase
  • Require cofactors: NAD‚Å∫, NADP‚Å∫, FAD

2. Transferases (EC 2)

  • Transfer functional groups between molecules
  • Examples: ALT, AST, Creatine kinase
  • Important in clinical diagnosis

3. Hydrolases (EC 3)

  • Catalyze hydrolysis reactions
  • Examples: Pepsin, Trypsin, Amylase
  • Important in digestion

4. Lyases (EC 4)

  • Add or remove groups to form double bonds
  • Examples: Aldolase, Carbonic anhydrase

5. Isomerases (EC 5)

  • Catalyze isomerization reactions
  • Examples: Glucose-6-phosphate isomerase

6. Ligases (EC 6)

  • Join molecules using ATP energy
  • Examples: DNA ligase, Acetyl-CoA carboxylase

Enzyme Kinetics

Michaelis-Menten Equation: V = (Vmax √ó [S]) / (Km + [S]) Where: V = Initial reaction velocity Vmax = Maximum velocity [S] = Substrate concentration Km = Michaelis constant

Key Parameters:

  • Km: Substrate concentration at half-maximal velocity
  • Vmax: Maximum reaction rate at substrate saturation
  • Kcat: Turnover number (reactions per second per enzyme)
  • Kcat/Km: Catalytic efficiency

Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity

1. Temperature

  • Increases activity until denaturation occurs
  • Q10 rule: Activity doubles for every 10¬∞C increase
  • Optimal temperature varies by enzyme

2. pH

  • Each enzyme has optimal pH range
  • Examples: Pepsin (pH 1.5-2.0), Trypsin (pH 8.0-8.5)
  • pH affects ionization of active site residues

3. Substrate Concentration

  • Follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics
  • Zero-order kinetics at high [S]
  • First-order kinetics at low [S]

4. Inhibitors

  • Competitive: Compete with substrate for active site
  • Non-competitive: Bind to allosteric site
  • Uncompetitive: Bind only to enzyme-substrate complex
Clinical Enzyme Markers:
  • ALT: Liver damage (hepatocytes)
  • AST: Liver and cardiac damage
  • CK: Muscle damage (skeletal and cardiac)
  • LDH: General tissue damage
  • Amylase: Pancreatic function
  • ALP: Liver and bone disorders
