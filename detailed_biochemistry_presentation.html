Introduction to Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the most abundant organic molecules on Earth and serve as the primary energy source for most living organisms. The name "carbohydrate" literally means "carbon with water."

  • Polysaccharides: Many monosaccharide units (starch, glycogen, cellulose)

Disaccharides - Linked Sugar Units

Disaccharides are formed when two monosaccharides are joined by a glycosidic bond through a dehydration reaction. They are important dietary components and metabolic intermediates.

üìã Major Disaccharides:

  • Sucrose: Glucose + Fructose (table sugar)
  • Lactose: Glucose + Galactose (milk sugar)
  • Maltose: Glucose + Glucose (malt sugar)
  • Trehalose: Glucose + Glucose (mushroom sugar)

üß™ Laboratory Analysis:

Disaccharide analysis involves:

  • Reducing sugar tests: Benedict's, Fehling's reagents
  • Enzymatic assays: Specific enzymes for each disaccharide
  • Chromatography: Separation and identification

Introduction to Proteins

Proteins are complex macromolecules composed of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. They are the most diverse and functionally important biological molecules.

üîß Protein Functions:

  • Catalytic: Enzymes accelerate biochemical reactions
  • Structural: Collagen, keratin provide mechanical support
  • Transport: Hemoglobin carries oxygen, albumin transports molecules
  • Defense: Antibodies protect against pathogens
  • Regulatory: Hormones like insulin control metabolism
  • Storage: Ferritin stores iron, casein stores amino acids
  • Movement: Actin and myosin enable muscle contraction

üè• Clinical Significance:

Protein analysis in medical laboratories includes:

  • Total protein: Nutritional and liver function assessment
  • Albumin: Liver synthesis and kidney function
  • Enzymes: Tissue damage markers (ALT, AST, CK)
  • Immunoglobulins: Immune system function

Protein Denaturation

Denaturation is the disruption of protein structure without breaking peptide bonds, resulting in loss of biological activity.

üå°Ô∏è Denaturing Agents:

  • Heat: Increases molecular motion, breaks weak interactions
  • pH extremes: Alters ionization states, disrupts salt bridges
  • Organic solvents: Disrupt hydrophobic interactions
  • Detergents: SDS disrupts protein structure
  • Chaotropic agents: Urea, guanidine HCl disrupt hydrogen bonds
  • Heavy metals: Bind to sulfur atoms, disrupt structure

üß™ Laboratory Applications:

  • Protein Precipitation: TCA, ammonium sulfate for purification
  • SDS-PAGE: SDS denatures proteins for electrophoresis
  • Urea-PAGE: Urea for native gel electrophoresis
  • Heat Denaturation: Coagulation studies, enzyme inactivation
  • Acid Precipitation: Protein removal from samples

üî¨ Reversible vs. Irreversible:

Reversible: Mild conditions, can refold (renaturation)

Irreversible: Harsh conditions, permanent loss of structure

Clinical relevance: Fever can denature enzymes, affecting metabolism

Enzymes - Biological Catalysts

Enzymes are proteins that catalyze biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy. They are essential for all metabolic processes.

üî¨ Enzyme-Substrate Interaction

‚ö° Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity:

  • Temperature: Increases activity until denaturation occurs
  • pH: Optimal pH for each enzyme (pepsin pH 2, trypsin pH 8)
  • Substrate concentration: Follows Michaelis-Menten kinetics
  • Inhibitors: Competitive, non-competitive, uncompetitive
  • Cofactors/Coenzymes: Required for activity (Mg¬≤‚Å∫, NAD‚Å∫)

üè• Clinical Enzyme Markers:

  • ALT (Alanine Transaminase): Liver damage marker
  • AST (Aspartate Transaminase): Liver and heart damage
  • CK (Creatine Kinase): Muscle damage marker
  • LDH (Lactate Dehydrogenase): Tissue damage marker
  • Amylase: Pancreatic function
